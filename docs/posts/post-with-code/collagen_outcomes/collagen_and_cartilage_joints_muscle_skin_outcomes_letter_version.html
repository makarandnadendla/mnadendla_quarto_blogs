I wanted to start this document off by noting that I could not find a SINGLE trial on collagen and airway cartilage outcomes. Not surprising, since you would probably need a long term cohort to evaluate this and you would need to account for a lot of variables for the adjustment set. So I tried to stick to things like skin, joint, and cartilage health outcomes in other places that I could find it. There are a few things that I think are essential for evaluating supplementary collagen, so I’ll list them out below:

  1. Assessment and control for protein intake at baseline, throughout the study, and between groups (especially in older adults). In particular and if possible, ensuring an slightly higher protein intake of at least 1.2g/kg lean mass in an isonitrogenic fashion between both arms. This one should be obvious since collagen obviously contributes to our amino acid pool when it’s digested, as well as the generally well known implications on increasing total protein intake for skin, bone, muscle, and connective tissue health especially in older adults. 

  2. Consistent dosages of the same molecules and consistent length of supplementation. Again, this one should be obvious, we want to be clear about what the estimand is.

  3. Either the collagen is a standalone intervention and compared to a placebo that’s PROTEIN MATCHED,

    OR have the treatment assignments be something like 

Treatment: Hydrolyzed Collagen + Hyaluronic Acid + Glucosamine + Condroiton

Control: Hyaluronic Acid + Condroitin + Glucosamine + Isonitrogenic Protein Supp

Let’s dive in, in no particular order I’ll be going over higher level meta analyses and systemic reviews and working my way down into commenting on individual trials.

Systematic Reviews and Meta Analysis

  • 2024 Meta Analysis/Systematic REview looking at a variety of outcome

    • “investigate the effects of long-term daily collagen peptide (CP) supplementation on strength, musculotendinous adaptation, functional recovery, and body composition in healthy adults”

    • The meta analysis authors used Standardized Mean Difference as the outcome measure. I maintain that most meta analyses nowadays shouldn’t be using it unless they can give a clear definition of the underlying construct that they’re trying to measure. The definition of the estimand for the meta analyses, is what leads to clear definitions of the construct used for the SMD. it’s a vital part of the process that most meta analyses using SMD don’t seem to do for whatever reason. SMD has almost no proper use case for a meta analysis these days and should only be used by a very very very experienced practitioner

    • Why is the second criteria of the meta analyses inclusion criteria that “they used different forms of DC (dietary collagen)”? If they simply mean using a variety of collagen molecules but in similar proportions and dosages across studies, I have no problem. But I’m not sure based on the wording here.(To be fair, many of the trials stuck to 15g daily DC supplementation). 

    • If I’m not mistaken, literally only one trial provided a protein matched placebo. Only ONE!!!!! The rest were some combination of maltodextrin, dextrose, or some random white powder that mixes similar to collagen in water.

    • They keep stacking people from the same study multiple times into the same forest plot. Wtf why??? Just make different subgroup analyses! THIS MAKES THE EFFECT MEASURE UNINTERPRETABLE. Not that SMD in this context wasn’t borderline uninterpretable from the start. Case in point:

My Final Conclusions on the Topic

Yes, glancing at these other trials, I’ve lost pretty much all faith that any of these can be redeemed. I need them to assess for baseline protein intake and have the placebo total protein matched AT A MINIMUM but I have only encountered like 2-3 that did that, and 2 of them assess MPS only and the lastt one I don’t have access to even with Scihub (also it’s an incredibly small study).  Thus almost none of them are equipped to interpret collagen’s effect on the body, independent of its protein. I think I’m done here, skimming over the characteristics of these trials in the systematic review as well as from searches I don’t think I can find a single one that’s redeemable for these outcomes in a super super robust way. There’s no like star studded trial guiding me here..

Update: My friend has informed me that there’s actually some decent evidence for isolated glycine (one of the primary amino acid constituents of collagen, along with proline and isoproline) for various endpoints. I’ll let him fetch some evidence on that and that could maybe offer any unique benefits of collagen if they are to be had for far cheaper. We shall see.

At present it’s not clear collagen is good for much beyond the protein content (except for maaaaybe skin aging if you’re a older woman with skin aging?), and I’d just recommend getting enough protein (daily 1.2 g protein/kg lean body mass, up to 1.6g/kg daily for older adults). The total protein intake is good for a lot of things health related. This is anecdotal, but if I had to guess the average Indian person gets only around 0.6g/kg protein. Maybe 0.8g/kg if we’re pushing it. That’s a major area in need of improvement. If we’re to use myself, I’m 75kg-ish, so multiply that by 1.2 and you get my daily protein target: ~90g. That’s hard to get by just winging it.

If you find 1.2g/kg hard, protein supplements are an excellent way to get up to that number. I personally have like 60g of soy protein in the morning and I know I’ll get the remaining 30g of protein throughout the day incidentally, just to make my life easier. I wouldn’t recommend nonveg proteins for more than like 15% of calories because of the implications for heart disease, and as you know India has a massive massive massive heart disease burden. Something like 60% of the world’s heart disease burden is from India??? Also I’m veg and biased, so yeah consume plant protein huehuehuehuehue

Hitting that number with just beans and legumes can be a little challenging and lead to a mono diet of those. Don’t get me wrong I love my pappu and it’s super healthy. Don’t stop eating kandipappu, pesarapappu, etc. But I think higher protein plant sources like soya (Meal Maker), tofu, tempeh, seitan, lupini beans are great (and I have them a lot), as well as protein supplements like soy protein isolate should be utilised more for the protein intake. I personally like seitan because it’s got an insanely high protein to calorie ratio and is economical to buy (people with Celiac’s, beware since seitan is literally just gluten). And protein powder, because it’s pretty tasty and I can make some tasty stuff. At worst you can buy like plain soy protein isolate and flavor that yourself to be economical, it’s very convenient because if like me you get a ton of protein in for like a shake or two you have the rest of your diet to be a bit more flexible without worrying about it as much. But my time in Japan and America has led to a deep deep appreciation for tofu and it’s far far more versatile than people think.
